Elementary Space Radius

In the framework of SPD-quantum gravity, space is quantized. The quanta of space are called elementary spaces, and their radius \(R_\mathrm{E}\) depends on the total mass energy of the particle — much like in general relativity, where the radius of a black hole depends on the total mass energy. Note that the space quanta of SPD are much larger than the Schwarzschild radii of particles in point-particle theories. Their radius is always larger than the Schwarzschild radius, and therefore elementary particles can never become black holes in this theory, regardless of by how much their energy is increased.

By comparing the diameter of the elementary space to the particle's wavelength, one can identify the critical energy at which the diameter becomes larger than the wavelength. When that happens, it becomes impossible to probe smaller distances with that particle. This explains why we don't discover any additional structures at scales below the critical energy characteristic to SPD — a phenomenon often called the particle desert. It is the reason why the LHC at CERN never discovered any new particles beyond the Higgs boson that was predicted in 1969.

That critical energy is in fact very close to the mass of the Higgs boson. Elementary spaces are used to explain W- and Z-bosons, as well as the Higgs boson. Merged elementary spaces are used to explain the three generations of matter. For details see The Complete Guide to Quantum Gravity (Sky Darmos; 2014–2026).

Note: Lengths are in metres (m), energies in joules (J), and masses in kilograms (kg).

The main formula

\[R_{\mathrm{E}} = \dfrac{2 \, G_{\mathrm{E}} \, E}{c^4}\]

\(R_\mathrm{E}\) = elementary space radius

\(G_\mathrm{E}\) = elementary space constant = \(k_{\mathrm{e}} \, e^2 \,/\, m_{\mathrm{p}}^2 = 8.246441821 \times 10^{25} \ \text{m}^3 \, \text{s}^{-2} \, \text{kg}^{-1}\)

\(E\) = energy of the particle (J)

\(c\) = speed of light in vacuum

\(k_{\mathrm{e}}\) = Coulomb constant

\(e\) = elementary charge

\(m_{\mathrm{p}}\) = proton mass

Formula for massive particles

For particles with mass, the energy in the main formula is the relativistic total energy, which depends on the particle's velocity v:

\[R_{\mathrm{E}} = \dfrac{2 \, G_{\mathrm{E}} \, \dfrac{m_{\mathrm{0}} \, c^2}{\sqrt{1 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2}}}}{c^4}\]

\(m_{\mathrm{0}}\) = rest mass of the particle (kg)

\(v\) = velocity of the particle

\(c\) = speed of light in vacuum

\(G_\mathrm{E}\) = elementary space constant

Formula for massless particles

For massless particles (photon, gluon), the elementary space radius depends on the frequency f or wavelength λ:

\[R_{\mathrm{E}} = \dfrac{2 \, G_{\mathrm{E}} \, h \, f}{c^4} = \dfrac{2 \, G_{\mathrm{E}} \, h}{c^3 \, \lambda}\]

\(f\) = frequency of the photon / gluon (Hz)

\(\lambda\) = wavelength (m)

\(h\) = Planck constant

\(c\) = speed of light in vacuum

\(G_\mathrm{E}\) = elementary space constant

The critical regime is reached when the elementary space diameter equals the wavelength (\(2R_\mathrm{E} = \lambda\)). The wavelength is given by \(\lambda = h \, c \,/\, E\) for massless particles, and by the de Broglie wavelength \(\lambda = h \, c \,/\, \sqrt{E^2 - m_{\mathrm{0}}^2 \, c^4}\) for massive particles.
